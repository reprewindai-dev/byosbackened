# Pilot Pricing Playbook — Internal Only

**Do not publish. Do not link from the website. Do not email this file.**

This is the private negotiating mechanic for the first 5–10 customer signings. The public website shows market rates only ($7,500 / $18,000 / $45,000). This document is what you use in private cold emails and contract addenda.

---

## The Mechanic

**60-Day Pilot Rate** — offered selectively to qualified institutions during private outreach.

| Tier | Public posted rate | Private pilot rate (60 days) | Day 61 onward |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Standard | $7,500/mo | **$1,500/mo** | $7,500/mo |
| Pro | $18,000/mo | **$4,000/mo** | $18,000/mo |
| Enterprise | $45,000/mo | **$9,500/mo** | $45,000/mo |

Pilot rate runs for 60 calendar days from contract execution. On day 61 the subscription auto-renews at posted market rate. Customer can cancel anytime (the perpetual license itself remains valid — they own the version they have).

**Cap: 10 institutions** total receive pilot pricing. After the tenth signs, this offer is permanently retired and not extended to anyone else.

---

## Who Qualifies for the Pilot Rate

You decide, not them. Use this as the gut-check filter:

- ✅ Logo you'd publicly name (regulated industry, recognizable, credible)
- ✅ Buyer is technical (CTO, VP Eng, Head of Platform, CISO) — not a procurement-only contact
- ✅ Real use case (not a tire-kicker fishing for free software)
- ✅ Willing to give you written permission to reference them as a customer
- ✅ Capable of paying market rate on day 61 (no startup with $200K MRR)

If they don't hit all five, they pay posted market rate or they don't sign.

## The Contract Mechanic

In the MSA / order form, the pilot rate is captured as an **introductory promotional rate** with an **automatic step-up** clause:

> *"For the first sixty (60) days from the Subscription Start Date, the Subscription Fee shall be $X per month (the "Introductory Rate"). Beginning on day sixty-one (61), the Subscription Fee shall automatically increase to the Standard Rate of $Y per month and shall continue at the Standard Rate for all subsequent months. Customer may terminate the Subscription at any time per the Termination clause; the perpetual license to the Software version delivered during the Subscription remains in effect regardless of termination."*

This is a perfectly normal commercial mechanic. Every enterprise software contract has language like this. Procurement teams have seen it a hundred times and don't blink.

---

## Why This Works (Why the Public Page Is Clean)

If we published the discount on the page, three bad things happen:

1. **It signals the higher rate isn't real.** Buyers think the $45K is a fake anchor. We lose pricing power.
2. **It invites negotiation on the discount itself.** "Can we have 90 days instead of 60?" "Can we get the discount for 12 months?" Now you're defending a number publicly instead of offering it privately as a favor.
3. **It cheapens the brand.** Premium positioning means the price card looks expensive. Discounts happen quietly.

By keeping the pilot off the page:

- Premium positioning intact
- Discount becomes a private gift you give to chosen logos (not an entitlement)
- Day-61 step-up is in their signed contract — they cannot pretend to be surprised
- After 10 signings, you simply stop offering it. No public retraction needed.
